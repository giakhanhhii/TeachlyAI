Parts of Speech

Every word in English belongs to a specific family.

The Big Three

Nouns

A

Words that name a person, place, thing, or idea. (e.g., Ocean, Fish, Beauty)

Verbs

B

Action words! They show what the noun is doing. (e.g., Swim, Splash, Dive)

Adjectives

C

Descriptive words that give more details about nouns. (e.g., Deep, Blue, Huge)

The Tenses

Time traveling with verbs: Past, Present, and Future.

🫧

Time Zones of English

Past

Yesterday

Things that already happened. We usually add -ed to regular verbs.
"The fish swamm away."

Now

Present

Things happening right now or general facts.
"The ocean is very deep."

Future

Tomorrow

Things that have not happened yet. We use will or going to.
"We will explore the reef."

Quick Grammar Quiz

What is "Ocean"?

It's a Noun because it is a naming word for a vast body of water.

Find the Adjective

"The friendly dolphin jumped."
Friendly is describing the dolphin!

What tense is "played"?

It is Past Tense because of the "-ed" ending at the end.

Find the Verb

"Sharks swim very fast."
Swim is the action word here.

Sentence Structure

How to construct perfect sentences like a master builder.

The Golden Rule: S - V - O

Most English sentences follow a strict order: Subject (who does the action), Verb (the action), and Object (who receives it).

The whale (S) eats (V) the plankton (O).

Sentence Types

Simple

Simple

Contains one independent clause with a subject and a verb. "The crab walks."

Compound

Compound

Two simple sentences joined by words like and, but, so. "The sun is hot, so we swim."

Complex

Complex

An independent clause plus a dependent clause. "Because it rained, the sea was rough."

Connecting Words

Conjunctions are like the glue of grammar! Words like and, but, or, because help connect small sentences into bigger, better ideas.

Grammar by the Numbers

50%

Verbs ending

Nearly 50% of verbs in past tense are regular (adding -ed).

75%

Subject First

In 75% of languages, the Subject comes before the Object.

25%

Exceptions

About 25% of English rules have weird exceptions. Just learn them!

THPTQG English 12

Let's dive from general grammar into exam-focused knowledge and smart test strategies.

🌿

Stress Rules Under the Sea

Quick stress guide
2 syllables
Nouns and adjectives often stress the first syllable, while many verbs stress the second. Example: TAble, HAPpy, beGIN.
Fixed endings
-tion, -sion, -ic often take stress before the ending. -ee, -eer, -ese usually stress the ending itself.
Exam trick
If you're unsure, read all four options silently and spot the word whose rhythm clearly differs from the other three.

Tense Signals You Must Remember

Present Simple
every day, always, often, usually, sometimes. Used for habits, routines and general truths.
Present Perfect
since, for, already, yet, recently, so far. Often confused with past simple in the exam.
Past Simple
yesterday, last week, ago, in 2024. Use it for actions completed at a clear time in the past.

Core Grammar Clusters

Passive Voice

be + V3/ed. Keep the original tense and only add “by” when the doer matters.

Reported Speech

Change pronouns, backshift tenses, and adjust time/place words like now → then, here → there.

Relative Clauses

Remember who, whom, which, whose, where, when and watch for reduced forms.

Conditionals

Type 1, 2, 3 and wish structures are all frequent grammar hotspots in THPTQG questions.

🐙

Word Form & Collocations

Nouns
Usually appear after articles, possessives or adjectives: the decision, his success, global education.
Adjectives
Common before nouns or after linking verbs: a successful student, become popular, feel nervous.
Collocations
Learn chunks such as make progress, take responsibility, do research, raise awareness.

Error Identification Steps

Step 1

SV

Check subject-verb agreement and find the real subject, not just the nearest noun.

Step 2

Tense

Use time expressions and sentence logic to test whether the tense is correct.

Step 3

Form

Look for word form, prepositions, comparisons, relative clauses and parallel structure.

Cloze Test Strategy

Read the Topic

Skim the title or first sentence to understand what the paragraph is mainly about.

Check Grammar

Look at the words before and after the blank to identify the needed part of speech.

Use Meaning

Eliminate options that do not fit the context, collocation, or connector relationship.

Read Again

Always reread the full paragraph to make sure the completed text sounds natural and logical.

Reading Comprehension

Main

Main Idea

Read the opening and closing of each paragraph to locate the central idea quickly.

Ref

Reference

Find which noun a pronoun such as it, they, this, these refers to in nearby lines.

Inf

Inference

Choose the answer supported by the text, not the one that only seems true outside the passage.

Sentence Transformation

Because
Because + clause can become Because of + noun / V-ing.
Although
Although + clause can become Despite / In spite of + noun / V-ing.
Too / Enough
too + adj + to V often transforms into not + adj opposite + enough + to V.

Communicative Functions

Invitation

Would you like to...? / Why don't we...? Answers: I'd love to / That sounds great.

Thanks & Apology

Thanks a lot → You're welcome. I'm sorry → Never mind / That's all right.

Agreement

I totally agree / That's true / I couldn't agree more.

Disagreement

I'm afraid I disagree / That's not always true / I don't think so.

Common Traps in the Exam

Negatives
Words like not, hardly, rarely, without can reverse the whole meaning if you read too fast.
Parallelism
Structures joined by and, or, but should keep the same grammatical form.
Familiar words
An answer containing the same word as the passage is not always correct. Check meaning and function carefully.
